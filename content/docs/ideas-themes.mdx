  <TabsContent value="dehumanisation">
    How Aboriginal People used to be treated has also been mentioned throughout the text, describing how torturous and inhumane such behaviours and attitudes were.
    <Accordions type="single">
      <Accordion value="dehumanisation-1" title={<span className="new-york" style={{ color: 'var(--color-fd-muted-foreground)', fontStyle: 'italic' }}>Danny looked over his shoulder briefly thinking he might see the lights of Cherbourg Mission behind the township, but there wasn't even a firefly's chance. Out of mind, out of sight.</span>}>
        The metaphor of there not being a firefly's chance represents the impossibility of noticing the Cherbourg mission. Representing how hopeless the situation is, with there not even being the smallest chance in the world, it brings up the theme attempt to erase Australia's dark past and treatment of Aboriginal people. The phrase, out of mind, out of sight, is a common saying when people want to forget about something, in this case the historical site of the Cherbourg Mission. The cruel conditions and methods of treatment of Aboriginal People the government wants erased from history signify how much Australia has changed yet fails to recognize and reconcile the poor handling that was faced.
      </Accordion>
      <Accordion value="dehumanisation-2" title={<span className="new-york" style={{ color: 'var(--color-fd-muted-foreground)', fontStyle: 'italic' }}>Bulls mother had seen the burial of too many Wakka Wakka kindred laid to rest in the Mission cemetery of sacred-red earth studded with white identical wooden crosses. The days of mourning and misery, misery and mourning, wave upon wave of Sorry Business, no justice and no reckoning for the innocent and the wretched.</span>}>
         Emotive language, repetition, and symbolism are all used to construct the theme of intergenerational Indigenous trauma. Too many Wakka Wakka kindred laid to rest suggests a history of systemic violence and neglect of Aboriginal people rather than individuals dying purely of natural causes. It signifies the amount of deaths as unnatural, and kindred describing how close they were in their own community, highlighting how it wasnâ€™t just a coincidence that brought about the death of many of their people. Furthermore, the description of white identical wooden crosses symbolizes colonial control, erasing individuality and cultural suppression, underscoring the idea of imposed religion and uniformity of Aboriginal people. Finally, the repetition of mourning and misery to describe the endlessness of grief and inability to move forward past times of neglect and dehumanisation, highlighting the lack of accountability from the Australian people.
